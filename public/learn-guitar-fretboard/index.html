Four-stage roadmap

Build knowledge you can use while playing.

Move forward when recall is reliable, not when you have merely read the next diagram.

01

Open strings and natural notes

Learn E A D G B E, then locate natural notes on strings 6 and 5. These two strings provide roots for common chords, scales, and barre shapes.

Daily test: Name ten random natural notes on strings 6 and 5 in under 30 seconds.
02

Octave shapes

Move a known note across two strings and two frets, adjusting for the B string. Say both note names while you play them.

Apply it: Find one C on string 5, then use octave relationships to locate the remaining Cs.
03

Intervals around a root

Choose a root and find its third, fourth, fifth, and seventh nearby. Listen to the character of each distance.

Pass when: You can play root–third–fifth in five keys without relying on one fixed shape.
04

Connect positions horizontally

Follow one scale along a single string, then connect adjacent vertical positions. This turns separate boxes into one continuous key map.

Practice: Use GuitarRun connection mode to move through three positions without stopping the pulse.
Common questions

Learning the fretboard FAQ

What is the best way to learn the guitar fretboard?

Use layers: open strings, natural-note landmarks, octave shapes, intervals, then scale degrees. Test recall by playing random targets rather than rereading diagrams.

Should I learn notes or scale shapes first?

Use both together. Shapes let you play quickly; note names and interval functions help you transpose, target harmony, and connect positions.

How much should I practice each day?

Ten focused minutes of active recall is enough to make progress. Stop before concentration falls and repeat the exercise the next day.

Why do the strings change at the B string?

Most adjacent guitar strings are tuned a perfect fourth apart, but G to B is a major third. Patterns crossing that boundary shift by one fret.
